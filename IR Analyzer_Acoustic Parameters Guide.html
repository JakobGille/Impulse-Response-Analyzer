Acoustic Parameters Guide

This reference document explains the physical calculations, psychoacoustic implications, and standard targets for all parameters and plots generated by the Impulse Response Analyzer.

Time & Decay Metrics

Early Decay Time (EDT)

The Concept: EDT measures how fast sound decays during the very first 10 dB of the drop, extrapolated to a full 60 dB decay. It is derived from the initial slope of the Schroeder integration.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: Unlike standard RT60 (which is a physical room constant), EDT correlates directly with the human perception of reverberance. Because the early decay happens while the sound is loudest, our auditory system relies on this brief temporal window to judge how "wet" or "dry" a room sounds.

Target: 0.2s - 0.4s (Studio Control Rooms) | 1.0s - 2.0s (Concert Halls)

Reverberation Time (RT60 / T20 / T30)

The Concept: The time it takes for a sound to decay by 60 dB. Because reaching a full 60 dB drop is mathematically rare due to background noise, it is extrapolated. T20 measures the drop from -5 dB to -25 dB. T30 measures from -5 dB to -35 dB.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: It represents the physical baseline of the room's acoustic energy storage. It defines the "tail" of the sound. T30 is highly robust against noise floor anomalies and represents the actual physical decay characteristics of the space.

Target: Strictly dependent on room volume and intended application.

Center Time (Ts)

The Concept: Calculates the temporal center of gravity of the squared impulse response.

Ts = ∫ (t * p²(t) dt) / ∫ p²(t) dt

Psychoacoustic Meaning: It is a measure of acoustic intimacy and transparency. A lower value means the energy is concentrated at the beginning (direct sound), making the source sound close, present, and highly defined. A high value means energy is spread heavily into the late reverb tail, pushing the sound source away from the listener.

Target: < 80 ms (Speech / High Definition) | 80 ms - 150 ms (Music)

Tonal Balance

Bass Ratio (BR) & Treble Ratio (TR)

The Concept: These ratios compare extrapolated reverberation times across standard octave bands.

  • Bass Ratio (BR): RT60 of low frequencies (125 Hz + 250 Hz) divided by mid frequencies (500 Hz + 1000 Hz).
  • Treble Ratio (TR): RT60 of high frequencies (2000 Hz + 4000 Hz) divided by mid frequencies.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: BR objectively quantifies acoustic warmth. TR quantifies brilliance. Rooms with an excessively high BR sound boomy and muddy. TR usually falls naturally below 1.0 because air molecules absorb high frequencies over distance.

Target BR: 1.1 - 1.25 (Classical warmth). Values > 1.5 indicate boominess.
Target TR: 0.8 - 1.0

Clarity & Definition

Clarity (C50 / C80)

The Concept: An early-to-late energy ratio expressed logarithmically in decibels. C50 compares energy before 50 ms to energy after 50 ms. C80 uses an 80 ms integration threshold.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: The human ear integrates early reflections with the direct sound, increasing perceived loudness and clarity. Reflections arriving late create reverberant masking. C50 is the benchmark for Speech Intelligibility (syllables are short). C80 is the benchmark for Music Clarity (musical notes are longer).

Target C50 (Speech): > 2 dB
Target C80 (Music): -2 dB to +4 dB (Higher for rhythmic music, lower for choral blending).

Definition (D50)

The Concept: Similar to C50, but expressed as a linear percentage instead of a logarithmic ratio. It measures the early energy (0-50 ms) relative to the total energy of the impulse response.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: Primarily used to evaluate syllable articulation. If D50 is exactly 50%, the early sound and the late reverberation have equivalent energetic weight.

Target: > 50% for good speech definition.

First Reflection Gap

The Concept: The absolute time delay (in milliseconds) between the arrival of the direct sound (the initial impulse peak) and the arrival of the very first distinct boundary reflection (floor, ceiling, or closest wall).

Psychoacoustic Meaning: To localize a sound accurately without tonal coloration, the auditory system requires an Initial Time Delay Gap (Haas effect). If strong reflections arrive too early, they cause destructive phase interference known as comb filtering, severely altering the frequency response of the direct sound.

Target: > 15 to 20 ms gap (Achieved in studios using Reflection Free Zones).

Spatial Parameters

Interaural Cross-Correlation (IACC)

The Concept: Calculated exclusively from Stereo files. It mathematically correlates the early sound (0-80 ms) arriving at the Left channel against the Right channel. 1.0 means identical signals; 0.0 means completely uncorrelated signals.

Psychoacoustic Meaning: IACC dictates the Apparent Source Width (ASW). Highly correlated signals sound like a narrow point source trapped inside the head. Highly uncorrelated early reflections widen the phantom source, giving the listener a sense of spaciousness.

Target: < 0.3 (Excellent envelopment) | > 0.6 (Narrow, poorly distributed soundfield).

Early Lateral Fraction (LF80)

The Concept: Calculated from Ambisonics B-Format signals. It is the ratio of early lateral energy (Y-Channel, Figure-8, 5-80 ms) to total early energy (W-Channel, Omni, 0-80 ms).

Psychoacoustic Meaning: Measures Listener Envelopment (LEV). The denominator includes the massive energy of the direct sound at 0 ms. The numerator excludes the direct sound (starts at 5ms) and captures only side reflections. Therefore, values must be relatively low. If values are too high, side reflections overpower the direct sound, destroying frontal localization.

Target: 0.10 to 0.35.
< 0.10 sounds like a dead, mono-like free-field.
> 0.35 causes loss of phantom source localization.

Visual Analysis & Plots

Waveform & Onset

What it shows: A time-domain plot of the raw amplitude of the impulse response (Mono or W-Channel). A red dotted vertical line explicitly marks the algorithmically detected onset.

How to read it: This plot verifies that the analysis is synchronized correctly. If the measurement contains a long pre-delay (silence before the actual sweep/clap), the red line indicates where the script identified the direct sound emerging from the noise floor, ensuring precise temporal calculations (C50/C80).

Broadband Energy Decay Curve

What it shows: The Schroeder integration curve plotted on a logarithmic (dB) scale over time. It visualizes the total energy leaving the room.

How to read it: A perfect room decay looks like a straight, smooth diagonal line. Visible "bumps" or sudden changes in the slope indicate acoustic anomalies, such as coupled spaces (sound escaping into a hallway and bouncing back) or flutter echoes. The curve abruptly stops where the ISO 3382 algorithm truncated the integration to prevent noise-floor contamination.

Octave Band Reverberation Time

What it shows: A bar chart displaying the calculated T30 (Reverberation Time) across six standard octave bands (125 Hz to 4 kHz).

How to read it: This reveals the tonal coloration of the reverb. A flat line means the room decays evenly. Tall bars on the left (125/250 Hz) indicate a room that traps low-end energy (common in small untreated rooms). Tall bars on the right indicate highly reflective surfaces (like glass or tile).

Frequency Response

What it shows: The spectral magnitude of the entire impulse response, calculated via a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). The bold line represents a 1/3 octave smoothing.

How to read it: This represents the "EQ curve" of the room. Deep, narrow dips (nulls) indicate phase cancellations caused by boundary reflections. Massive peaks in the low end (below 200 Hz) highlight resonant room modes (standing waves).

Spectrogram (Top-Down Waterfall)

What it shows: A 2D time-frequency representation. Time is on the X-axis, frequency is on the Y-axis (logarithmic), and color intensity represents acoustic energy (dB).

How to read it: This plot combines the frequency response and the decay curve. It shows exactly which frequencies ring out the longest. A long, bright horizontal streak at 100 Hz clearly identifies a problematic, lingering room resonance.

Directional Energy Mapping (Ambisonics Only)

What it shows: Active intensity vector mapping derived from B-Format signals (W, X, Y, Z).

  • Horizontal Heatmap (Azimuth vs. Time): Shows when early reflections arrive (0-100ms) and from which angle on the horizontal plane. 0° is front, ±90° are the sides, ±180° is the rear.
  • 3D Spherical Heatmap (Mollweide): An equirectangular projection mapping Azimuth (X-axis) against Elevation (Y-axis).

How to read it: These plots act as an acoustic radar. A bright spot on the horizontal map at 20ms / 90° points precisely to a strong reflection off the left wall. A bright spot on the Mollweide map directly above the center (0° Azimuth, +90° Elevation) identifies an untreated ceiling reflection.
